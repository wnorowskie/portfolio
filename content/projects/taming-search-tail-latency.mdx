# Summary

Our Elasticsearch-powered search API serves 75,000+ customers against a corpus of 360k+ curated projects. Typical queries were fast — but the worst-case patterns, cross-entity searches with heavy mixed filters, had a tail that told a different story: **p95 around 1.5 seconds and p99 around 9.4 seconds** under load. This is the story of redesigning cross-search to bring that p99 down to ~1.4 seconds, and of the production memory incident along the way that hardened how we ship.

## The Problem

Percentiles hide sins. Our median cross-search latency looked respectable (~545ms), but the p99 was ~9.4s — meaning the unluckiest searches, often the most complex and valuable ones, were effectively timing out from the user's perspective. The culprit patterns combined searches across entity types with worst-case filter mixes that the original query construction handled by doing too much work in too few places.

## The Redesign

We rebuilt the cross-search implementation around the query shapes that actually hurt, and validated it the same way we found the problem — synthetic load campaigns (k6) tied to production traffic levels:

- **Stress scenario** (variable load, up to ~50 RPS): p50 ~200ms, p95 ~1.0s, **p99 ~1.4s** — the 9.4s tail cut by ~85%
- **Steady load scenario**: p50 ~120ms, **p95 ~250ms**, p99 ~350ms
- **Zero failures** — no 5xx errors or timeouts across 220k+ requests
- Test throughput ran at up to **~8× production peak**, so the headroom is measured, not hoped for

The same testing discipline extends to relevance work: before shipping hybrid ranking (RRF) and proximity search, we quantified their latency cost under load — proximity turned out to be ~1% overhead; hybrid ranking's cost was real and became an explicit relevance-vs-latency decision rather than a surprise.

## The Incident

Mid-rollout, a production deploy sent memory climbing to 100% on the search service. We rolled back the same day, then dug in. Root cause: for large responses, the service was **building entire NDJSON payloads as huge in-memory strings**, creating large-object-heap pressure that accumulated until the service tipped over.

The fix was structural, not a tuning knob:

1. **Stream NDJSON directly to the response** instead of materializing it — memory now rises and falls proportionally to load
2. **Cap oversized requests to fail fast**, so a pathological request degrades one response instead of the whole service

We redeployed a week later with load tests confirming no recurrence.

## Lessons

- **Chase the tail, not the median.** The p99 is where your most complex — often most valuable — queries live.
- **Load-test at multiples of production, and load-test the worst case.** Well-shaped queries told us nothing; the pathological mixes were the whole story.
- **A same-day rollback is a feature.** Deploy checklists with rollback plans meant the incident cost hours, not days — and the root cause got a durable fix instead of a patch under pressure.
- **Quantify tradeoffs before shipping them.** Relevance improvements that cost latency should be decisions, not discoveries.
